Geography and the Internet: A Close Reading

by Bahle Jacqueline (Jackie) Sibanda on 13th March 2025

A critical examination of Greenstein, Forman, and Goldfarb's "How Geography Shapes and Is Shaped by the Internet" and its challenge to techno-utopian views of internet democratization.

CHALLENGING THE "DEATH OF DISTANCE" THESIS

In their article, Greenstein, Forman and Goldfarb (2018) offer a well-researched challenge to the belief that the internet flattens geography. Instead of viewing digital networks as neutral forces that transcend physical location, they argue that the development of the internet in the United States during the 1990s-early 2000s was deeply embedded in the country's geographic and economic landscape. Through careful analysis and historical insight, they demonstrate a reciprocal relationship where geography affected the internet's expansion, while the internet simultaneously reshaped geography in ways that reinforced existing inequalities.

RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

Challenging techno-utopian narratives of spatial democratization, Greenstein et al. (2018) set out to determine whether the internet actually replaced the importance of geography or if it made geographical differences more deep-seated. Methodologically, the article relies on reduced-form econometric analysis using county data from the 1990s. They analyze the behavior of ISPs, mapping these decisions onto variables like population density, median income, telecommunications infrastructure, and proximity to urban centers.

KEY FINDINGS

One of the article's most significant contributions lies in its refutation of the "death of distance" thesis. The research shows that early internet rollout was highly correlated with traditional geographic advantages. Counties with higher populations and incomes attracted better ISPs while rural areas were overlooked due to weak market incentives. In many cases, underserved areas not only faced late entry of ISPs but also limited competition when access was established. Fewer providers meant higher prices and slower service, which widened the digital sectionide. Rather than leveling the playing field, the internet accelerated the economic growth of already thriving communities.

CONTEMPORARY RELEVANCE AND LIMITATIONS

The result was a feedback loop in which disadvantage compounds over time. While the article focuses on the early emergence of the internet, its implications remain relevant in today's policy debates around digital access. One possible limitation is the historical scope - since the early 2000s, technologies like wireless broadband, satellite internet, and mobile data have transformed connectivity. These may have altered some structural barriers mentioned in the article. Yet enduring inequalities in digital access, particularly those evident during the COVID-19 pandemic, suggest that the dynamics uncovered by Greenstein et al. (2018) continue to shape the digital sectionide.

CONCLUSION

Ultimately, the article succeeds in offering an empirically grounded pushback against optimistic visions of the internet's power to overcome spatial limitations. Greenstein, Forman, and Goldfarb reveal how the early internet was not an equalizer but a mirror that amplified existing inequalities. They highlight the importance of viewing technology in historical, geographical and economic context. As debates over broadband access and digital equity continue, this article remains an essential reference point for scholars and policymakers.

REFERENCES

Greenstein, S., Forman, C. and Goldfarb, A. (2018) How Geography Shapes—and Is Shaped by—the Internet. Edited by G.L. Clark et al. Oxford University Press. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordhb/9780198755609.013.21
